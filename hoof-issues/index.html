Top 10 Hoof Issues
for Horse Owners

The most common hoof problems you will face — with complete guides covering what each condition is, how your farrier treats it, when to call your vet, and what you can do at home every day.

Know What You're Dealing With

From thrush and laminitis to abscesses, cracked hooves, canker, and scratches (mud fever), most horse hoof problems are predictable, preventable, and treatable — if you catch them early and know which role belongs to whom. Each guide below explains what the condition is, what causes it, how to recognize the symptoms, the farrier's approach, the veterinarian's role, and the daily steps every horse owner can take to treat and prevent it.

Complete Guides — Every Condition Covered in Depth
1
Thrush
Thrush is the most common hoof infection — a bacterial invasion of the frog's grooves that produces black, foul-smelling discharge. Entirely preventable, easily treated when caught early, and potentially serious if ignored.
Mild to Serious Very Common
→
2
White Line Disease
White line disease (seedy toe) is a fungal and bacterial invasion of the white line zone where hoof wall meets sole. It can silently undermine and separate large sections of hoof wall before the horse ever shows lameness.
Mild to Severe Common
→
3
Laminitis & Founder
Laminitis (and its advanced form, founder) is inflammation of the sensitive laminae — one of the most serious and painful hoof conditions a horse can develop. It demands immediate veterinary attention, and fast recognition of the symptoms changes everything.
Serious Common
→
4
Navicular Syndrome
Navicular syndrome is degeneration of the navicular bone and surrounding structures — a leading cause of chronic forelimb lameness in performance horses. It is manageable for years with the right farrier and veterinary team and correct shoeing.
Serious Common
→
5
Cracked Hooves
Cracked hooves range from superficial grass cracks to deep quarter cracks that bleed and cause lameness. All six crack types explained, with farrier repair techniques, infection management, and how to prevent splits and chips from returning.
Mild to Serious Common
→
6
Hoof Abscesses
A hoof abscess causes sudden, severe lameness from a pocket of pressure-building infection trapped inside the hoof. One of the most dramatic yet most treatable causes of acute foot pain — a horse can go sound to three-legged lame overnight.
Moderate Common
→
7
Contracted Heels
Contracted heels are a progressive narrowing of the heel region — a hidden root cause of chronic thrush, abscesses, navicular syndrome, and poor performance. Often reversible with patient farrier rehabilitation over 9–18 months.
Moderate Common
→
8
Thin Soles
Thin soles (flat feet) leave inadequate sole depth between the coffin bone and the ground, causing bruising and footiness on hard or rocky terrain. Common in Thoroughbreds and post-laminitis horses, and manageable with correct farrier protection and a long-term sole-building strategy.
Mild to Moderate Common
→
9
Canker
Canker (proliferative pododermatitis) is an abnormal overgrowth of frog tissue that looks and smells like thrush but is the opposite disease — and far more serious. Frequently misdiagnosed; needs coordinated farrier and veterinary treatment.
Serious Uncommon
→
+
Scratches (Pastern Dermatitis)
Scratches — also called mud fever, greasy heel, or dew poisoning — is scabby, crusty inflammation on the back of the pastern driven by the wet-mud cycle. Easy to clear early, stubborn once chronic. Cleaning, treatment, and prevention.
Mild to Moderate Common
→
