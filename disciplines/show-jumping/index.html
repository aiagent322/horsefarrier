Show Jumping

In show jumping, a horse that slips on takeoff or landing is a safety crisis — for horse and rider. Traction is the dominant concern, balanced against shoe weight and the need for consistent, predictable performance across varying arena and grass surfaces.

Rim Shoes and Stud Shoes

The two standard shoe types in show jumping are rim shoes and stud shoes. Rim shoes have a raised rim on the ground surface that grips turf and arena footing consistently without requiring individual stud selection — the shoe provides traction in all directions. Stud shoes are standard or rim shoes with pre-tapped threaded holes in the heel and/or toe positions that accept removable threaded studs. The rider or groom swaps studs for each surface: small road studs for dry firm grass, medium grass studs for normal going, large or pointed studs for deep or wet conditions. Many top competitors carry multiple sets and change studs between rounds.

  • Standard shoe: Rim shoe (steel or aluminum) for most arena and grass work
  • Stud holes: Typically 2 holes — one each side at the heel; some shoes have 4 holes
  • Stud removal: Holes packed with cotton wool between uses to prevent thread damage
  • Hind priority: Traction on the hind feet matters most — that is where the horse drives off
  • Front shoe weight: Aluminum fronts are common in sport horses to reduce fatigue

Stud Selection by Footing

Stud selection is a skill in itself, and top riders take it seriously. Road studs are small, bullet-shaped projections for firm dry ground — too large a stud on hard ground torques the limb on landing. Grass studs are broader and square-ended, for normal-to-soft going. Mud studs are long and pointed, penetrating deep going to find purchase underneath. Dome studs (round-topped) are used when the ground is variable — they provide grip without the torque risk of sharp studs on inconsistent surfaces. A critical rule: always use the same size stud on both sides of a shoe — asymmetric studs create uneven loading that stresses joints.

  • Road studs: Firm dry footing — small, bullet-shaped
  • Grass studs: Normal going — broader, square-ended
  • Mud studs: Deep going — long, pointed
  • Dome studs: Variable/inconsistent ground — round top, moderate penetration
  • Key rule: Always match stud size medially and laterally on the same shoe

Hoof Balance and Breakover in Jumping

Beyond traction, hoof balance is critical in jumping because every takeoff and landing puts enormous concentrated force through the lower limb. A long toe or low heel in a jumping horse increases DDFT tension at every takeoff — multiply that by hundreds of fences across a season and the cumulative damage is significant. Many top jumping farriers apply a rolled toe or rocker toe on front shoes to ease breakover and reduce DDFT tension, even on otherwise sound horses. The combination of correct hoof-pastern axis, appropriate breakover, and matched traction is the complete picture of jumping farriery — traction alone is insufficient.

  • Common modification: Rolled or squared toe on fronts to ease breakover
  • Balance priority: Hoof-pastern axis must be straight — broken-back HPA in a jumping horse is a welfare concern
  • Reset interval: 6 weeks typically; closer to competition, some riders prefer 5 weeks
  • Shoe security: Quarter or toe clips prevent shoe shifting on landing impact
