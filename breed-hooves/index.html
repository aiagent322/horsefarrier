Breed-Specific Hoof Needs

Every breed has distinct hoof characteristics. Find yours and learn what it means for trimming intervals, shoe selection, and common problems to watch for.

Hoof quality, shape, and common problems vary significantly by breed — the result of genetics, selective breeding priorities, and the original environments these horses evolved in. What works for a Thoroughbred may be wrong for a Draft horse. This guide covers the most common breeds encountered by North American horse owners.

Light Horse Breeds
American Quarter Horse
Moderate hoof quality

Hoof Characteristics

  • Compact, round hoof
  • Thick, dense hoof wall
  • Well-developed frog
  • Generally good quality

Common Problems

  • Contracted heels (especially in stalled horses)
  • Founder susceptibility in easy keepers
  • White line disease in wet climates

Farrier Notes

Generally easy to shoe. Performance horses often benefit from sliding plates or bell boots for protection. Monitor diet closely — easy keepers are laminitis-prone.

Thoroughbred
Softer, thinner hooves

Hoof Characteristics

  • Larger, flatter hoof
  • Thinner hoof wall
  • Less concavity in sole
  • Often more brittle

Common Problems

  • Thin soles — bruising on hard ground
  • Cracking and chipping of wall
  • Nail holes weakening the wall
  • Quarter cracks under stress

Farrier Notes

Nail placement is critical with thin walls. Many TBs benefit from hoof supplements. Some work better in rim pads or with pour-in pads for sole protection. Tighter trim intervals often needed.

Arabian
Generally hard, dense hooves

Hoof Characteristics

  • Smaller, dense, oval hoof
  • Naturally hard wall
  • Well-arched sole
  • Compact, efficient structure

Common Problems

  • Small hooves under-support body mass in heavy individuals
  • Upright "club" foot tendency in some lines
  • Abscesses in very dry conditions

Farrier Notes

Often the easiest breed to maintain barefoot. The naturally hard hoof can be difficult to nail through — sharp nails and properly placed nail holes are essential. Many Arabians go indefinitely without shoes.

Mustang / Wild-type
Exceptionally hard hooves

Hoof Characteristics

  • Tight, concave, self-maintaining
  • Extremely hard wall
  • Thick, callused sole
  • Well-developed digital cushion

Common Problems

  • Difficulty transitioning to soft footing
  • Hard to nail — wall resists
  • Long adjustment period after domestication

Farrier Notes

Mustangs and Mustang crosses are often held up as the ideal for barefoot advocates — their naturally selected hooves are remarkably strong. Minimal intervention is often the best approach. Many never need shoes.

Warmblood (KWPN, Hanoverian, etc.)
Variable — breed-dependent

Hoof Characteristics

  • Large hooves relative to body size
  • Varies widely by breeding
  • Some lines have excellent quality; others are soft

Common Problems

  • White line disease in wet climates
  • Thrush in large, open frogs
  • Some lines prone to thin soles

Farrier Notes

Sport horses do significant work on hard arena surfaces — hoof protection is usually necessary. A good farrier familiar with sport horse disciplines is worth finding. Hoof supplements are widely used in this discipline.

Tennessee Walking Horse
Modified by discipline

Hoof Characteristics

  • Naturally moderate quality
  • Show horses grown to extreme lengths
  • Trail horses often go naturally

Common Problems

  • Show practices (pads, chains) create welfare concerns
  • Long toes stress the deep digital flexor tendon
  • Soring history affects regulatory environment

Farrier Notes

Natural TWHs kept for trail or pleasure work are easy to maintain barefoot or with standard shoeing. The natural gait emerges without the extreme shoeing packages used in show rings. Avoid long toes regardless of discipline.

Draft & Heavy Breeds
Draft Breeds (Clydesdale, Percheron, Belgian)
Soft, feathered hooves

Hoof Characteristics

  • Very large, wide hooves
  • Feathering traps moisture
  • Softer wall than light breeds
  • Large frog and digital cushion

Common Problems

  • Chronic progressive lymphedema (CPL)
  • Scratches / mud fever in pasterns
  • Thrush from large moist frog
  • Difficulty maintaining balance in heavy individuals

Farrier Notes

Draft farriery is physically demanding and requires specialized large shoes. The feathering (hair) must be kept clean and dry to prevent scratches. Many draft owners clip the feathers for easier management. Larger nails and nail counts typical.

Pony Breeds (Welsh, Shetland, Connemara)
Hardy, hard hooves

Hoof Characteristics

  • Compact, very hard hooves
  • Excellent natural quality
  • Strong, self-maintaining in many cases

Common Problems

  • Extreme laminitis susceptibility — easy keeper metabolism
  • Overgrown hooves if neglected
  • Grass founder in spring and fall

Farrier Notes

Ponies have evolved on sparse, rough terrain — their hooves reflect it. Many go barefoot their entire lives. However, diet management is critical: laminitis in ponies kills. Grazing muzzles and dry lots are essential tools.

A Note on Individual Variation

Breed tendencies are a starting point, not a prescription. Individual horses within any breed vary enormously — influenced by parentage, diet, environment, workload, and management history. The best farrier approach is always based on what's in front of you, not what the breed "usually" looks like. Use these profiles to know what to watch for, then let your farrier's eyes make the call.
