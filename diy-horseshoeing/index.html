DIY Horseshoeing — The Owner's Guide

What you can safely do yourself, what you shouldn't attempt, the tools you need, and how to pull a shoe, trim, nail, and clinch — step by step.

The Honest Assessment

DIY horseshoeing is a real thing. Thousands of horse owners in remote areas, on tight budgets, or with the time and commitment to learn, successfully maintain their horses' feet between professional farrier visits — or in some cases, entirely. It is skilled work that takes time to learn, carries genuine risks if done poorly, and is emphatically not a substitute for a qualified farrier on horses with complex needs.

This guide is written for owners who want to learn what they can legitimately do themselves, understand where the limits are, and handle emergency situations competently. It is not a guide to replacing your farrier entirely on performance horses, horses with hoof health issues, or horses that need the work of a trained professional.

The honest starting point: if you have access to a competent farrier, use them. The skills described on this page take months of practice to develop to a level where they reliably help rather than harm. They are worth learning as a backup, as a supplement, and for the understanding they give you of what your farrier does. They are not a shortcut.

Learn in Person First

Everything on this page is more safely learned from a person than from a screen. Ask your farrier to explain what they're doing as they work. Many farriers will let an engaged owner watch and ask questions. Some will show you how to pull a shoe or rasp a small flare if you express genuine interest in learning. A YouTube video can supplement that instruction — it cannot replace it.

What Owners Can and Cannot Do

The line is not about courage or motivation — it's about the consequences of error and the skill required to avoid them.

✓ Reasonable Owner Territory
  • Pulling a loose or sprung shoe safely to prevent further injury
  • Basic maintenance trimming on horses with healthy, well-established hooves between farrier visits
  • Rasping small flares or sharp edges that appear between appointments
  • Applying a hoof boot after a lost shoe
  • Picking out and cleaning hooves daily
  • Applying basic hoof dressing or thrush treatment
  • Tightening a risen clinch temporarily with a clinch block and hammer
  • Resetting a shoe on a sound horse with good hoof wall after learning from a professional
✗ Leave It to the Farrier
  • Any horse with a lameness condition — laminitis, navicular, tendon injury
  • Corrective or therapeutic shoeing of any kind
  • Foals and young horses — developmental trimming is a specialty
  • Horses with thin soles, contracted heels, or chronic hoof quality problems
  • Quarter cracks or any crack extending into sensitive tissue
  • Any situation where the horse is in pain before you start
  • Forging or shaping custom shoes
  • Placing nails on a horse that is difficult to handle for the farrier
The Rule of Thumb

If the horse is lame, if you're not sure, or if something looks wrong — stop and call the farrier or vet. The cost of a professional visit is small compared to the cost of making a problem worse. DIY horseshoeing earns its value in routine maintenance situations, not in complex or uncertain ones.

Tools You Need

A basic owner toolkit is not the same as a professional farrier's kit — you don't need a forge, an anvil, or a full apron of specialty tools. But you do need specific equipment, and quality matters. Cheap tools make the work harder, less precise, and more likely to cause injury to the hoof or to you.

Ask Your Farrier What They Use

Before buying tools, ask your farrier what shoe size, nail size, and rasp brand they use on your horse. Buying compatible consumables means your emergency reset uses the same geometry as the professional work — not an approximation that may not fit.

How to Pull a Shoe Safely

Pulling a shoe is the most important emergency skill an owner can have. A sprung, loose, or partially pulled shoe is a serious injury risk — it can catch on things, torque the hoof wall, or be stepped on by another foot. Every owner who has a shod horse should know how to pull a shoe safely.

The critical rule: always cut the clinches before attempting to pull the shoe. Attempting to pull a shoe with intact clinches tears chunks of hoof wall off with it. The clinches must be opened first.

1
Secure the Horse
Tie the horse safely or have someone hold them. You need both hands for shoe removal. Do not attempt this with a horse that is loose or that you cannot safely handle with its foot up.
2
Pick Up the Foot and Assess
Pick up the foot and look at the shoe. Count the clinches — typically 6 to 8 on a standard shoe. Note whether any have already risen (standing up from the hoof wall) — these need to be knocked flat before cutting. A risen clinch can cut you when you run the clinch cutter along the wall.
3
Knock Down Any Risen Clinches
If any clinches are standing up, hold the clinching block against the nail head on the underside of the shoe and tap the risen clinch flat with your hammer. Do not cut risen clinches — cut them after they are flat against the wall.
4
Cut Every Clinch
Place the beveled edge of the clinch cutter under each clinch — between the bent nail tip and the hoof wall — and strike it firmly with the hammer to cut through or open the clinch. Work around all clinches systematically. Do not miss any — a single uncut clinch will tear the wall when you pull the shoe. Run a finger along the wall when done to confirm all clinches are open and flat.
5
Start at One Heel with the Pull-offs
Place the jaws of the pull-offs at one heel of the shoe, with the curved jaws working under the shoe from the heel branch. Lever slowly toward the toe — do not yank. The goal is to break each nail free progressively, not to rip the shoe off. Move from one heel to the other heel alternately, working toward the toe in small increments.
6
Work Both Heels Toward the Toe
Alternate from heel to heel, never removing the shoe from only one side — this creates torque that can crack the hoof. Each lever motion frees one or two nails. The shoe will loosen progressively. When it lifts freely from the toe, it is ready to remove.
7
Remove the Shoe and Inspect the Hoof
Slide the shoe forward off the toe and set it aside. Inspect the hoof wall for any tearing or chunks of wall removed with the shoe — this happens when clinches were not fully cut. Look at the nail holes — they should be clean punched holes. Any torn, ragged, or missing wall sections should be assessed by your farrier before the horse is reshod. Apply a hoof boot immediately to protect the bare foot.
After Pulling a Shoe

A horse with one bare foot and three shod feet has a significant height imbalance — the bare foot is now effectively lower than the others, altering the horse's posture, loading patterns, and potentially causing discomfort. Apply a hoof boot to the bare foot immediately and call your farrier. Do not leave a horse in this state for longer than necessary.

Basic Maintenance Trimming

Basic maintenance trimming — keeping a barefoot horse's hooves from overgrowing between professional visits, or knocking back a small flare on a shod horse — is within reach of a motivated owner who has learned the fundamentals in person. Full corrective trimming is not.

The fundamental objectives of a maintenance trim:

  • Maintain the hoof at a length where the sole is approximately level with the wall — the wall should not project significantly below the sole
  • Maintain balanced medial-lateral (side-to-side) height — both sides of the hoof at the same level
  • Maintain a hoof-pastern axis that is straight — not broken forward or backward
  • Remove flares — sections of hoof wall that have bent outward — by rasping the outer wall only
  • Keep the mustang roll (for barefoot horses) — a beveled edge on the bottom of the hoof wall that prevents chipping and cracking
1
Observe Before You Touch
Stand back and look at the horse from in front, behind, and both sides before picking up a foot. Note whether the hooves look balanced side to side, whether the hoof-pastern angle looks correct, and whether any obvious flares are visible. Trimming is easier to get right when you understand what you're looking at before you start.
2
Pick Up, Clean, and Assess
Clean the foot thoroughly with a hoof pick. Look at the sole — is it flat, slightly concave? Is there loose or flaky sole material? Look at the frog — is it healthy, firm, free of deep sulci (grooves) with dark material? Look at the wall — where is it long? Where is it flared? Identify exactly what needs doing before rasping anything.
3
Rasp the Bearing Surface Level
Place the rasp flat on the bearing surface of the hoof wall and rasp to remove excess length, keeping the rasp as level as possible. Work in long strokes from heel to toe. Check frequently by setting the foot down and looking at the hoof from behind to confirm both heels remain at the same height. Do not try to remove a lot at once — small incremental passes are safer and more controlled.
4
Address Flares on the Outer Wall
A flare is a section of outer wall that has bent outward — the wall is no longer following the angle of the upper hoof. Rasp the outer wall only (not the bearing surface) at the flared section to bring the wall back to the correct angle. Use the coronary band angle as your guide — the wall should parallel it from top to bottom. Never rasp so aggressively that you expose white wall or sensitive tissue.
5
Apply the Mustang Roll (Barefoot Horses)
For a barefoot horse, bevel the bottom edge of the hoof wall all the way around — a 45-degree bevel from the flat bearing surface up to about a third of the way up the outer wall. This prevents the sharp edge from chipping and cracking when it contacts the ground. Use the rasp at an angle and work around the entire perimeter. This is the single most effective thing you can do to prevent chipping and cracking in barefoot horses.
6
Set the Foot Down and Check
After trimming each foot, set it down and watch how the horse stands and how the foot contacts the ground. Step back and look from in front and behind to check medial-lateral balance. Check the hoof-pastern axis from the side. If something looks uneven, pick the foot back up and adjust. Do not leave an obvious imbalance because you've already spent time on that foot — an imbalanced trim is worse than an untrimmed hoof.
Less Is More, Always

In owner trimming, the most common damaging mistake is removing too much. You can always rasp more — you cannot put material back. If you're uncertain whether a hoof needs more removed, stop and look again at the horse standing. If there is any doubt, leave it for the farrier. A maintenance trim's goal is to prevent overgrowth and address obvious flares, not to achieve the precision balance of a professional trim.

Nailing on a Shoe

Nailing on a shoe is the skill with the highest consequences for error. A nail placed too close to the white line — a "close nail" — invades the sensitive laminar tissue and causes lameness that can last days to weeks. A nail driven in the wrong direction exits the hoof wall in the wrong location and must be removed immediately. Getting this right requires practice on dummy hooves before attempting it on a living horse.

Before You Start

  • Confirm the shoe fits the hoof correctly — it should cover the entire bearing surface of the hoof wall with no overhang inward and appropriate coverage at the heels. A shoe that doesn't fit properly cannot be corrected by nailing it on.
  • Have your nails ready — the correct size for this shoe. Nails that are too long will exit the wall too high; too short will not clinch adequately.
  • Confirm the horse is calm, standing on level ground, and that you can safely hold the foot in position through the nailing process.
1
Position the Shoe and Start the First Nail
Hold the shoe in position against the trimmed hoof wall. Start with a nail in the front quarter of the shoe — not the toe nail hole, but the first hole back from the toe on one side. The nail should angle slightly outward (away from the center of the hoof) so it exits the wall at the correct height — typically ¾ to 1 inch above the shoe. Drive the nail until it is secure but do not fully seat it yet.
2
Drive the Opposing Nail
Place a nail in the corresponding hole on the opposite side of the shoe. This locks the shoe in position. With two nails roughly placed — one on each side — you can now confirm the shoe is sitting where you want it before committing all nails. Check that the shoe covers the heels and is centered on the hoof.
3
Drive All Remaining Nails
Work around the shoe, alternating sides, driving each nail to where the tip just exits the hoof wall. Watch for the nail tip as it exits — it should emerge at consistent height on both sides in a straight horizontal line. A nail exiting too high suggests the angle was too shallow. A nail that doesn't exit or exits very low suggests it may have been driven too steeply — remove it immediately and probe the nail hole with a pick to see if laminar tissue has been entered (the horse will react).
4
Clinch Off Nail Tips
Once all nails are driven, break off the nail tips (the exposed points above the wall) by twisting with the pull-offs. Do not leave nail points exposed — they are a serious laceration hazard to you, the horse, and anyone nearby. The break should leave a short stub of nail protruding from the wall for clinching.
5
Seat the Shoe Fully
With all nails broken off and stubs protruding, drive each nail head down firmly against the shoe with the hammer so the shoe sits flat against the bearing surface with no gaps. Go around all nails to confirm full seating.
The Close Nail — Know the Signs

If a nail has been placed too close to sensitive tissue, the horse will often react when the nail is driven — pulling the foot away, vocalizing, or becoming suddenly fractious. If this happens, remove the nail immediately. Do not assume the horse will "settle." After removing a close nail, the horse may or may not be lame. If there is any doubt, call your vet — a close nail that's been left in for even a short time can introduce infection into sensitive tissue.

Clinching the Nails

Clinching — bending the nail stubs flat against the hoof wall to lock the shoe in place — is the final step in shoeing and the one that determines how securely the shoe stays on. A well-clinched shoe with good clinches will stay on for the full shoeing cycle. Poorly clinched nails rise over time, become sharp and potentially dangerous, and allow the shoe to shift or loosen.

1
Rasp a Small Notch at Each Nail Stub
Using the rasp, make a small horizontal groove in the hoof wall directly below each nail stub. This notch gives the clinch somewhere to seat into — without it, the bent nail sits on the surface of the wall and is more likely to rise. This step is what distinguishes a professional clinch from a beginner one.
2
Hold the Clinching Block Against the Nail Head
With the foot on the ground or supported on a stand, hold the clinching block firmly against the nail head on the bottom of the shoe. The block provides the resistance that allows you to bend the stub over without simply pushing the nail sideways. This requires a helper or good technique for hind feet.
3
Bend Each Stub Over with the Hammer
Strike the nail stub sideways with the hammer to bend it over into the notch you made. Strike toward the hoof — not away from it. The stub bends down and into the wall surface. Work around all nails, checking that each clinch is bending toward the wall rather than standing proud of it.
4
Flatten Each Clinch Tightly
Go around all clinches a second time, striking firmly to flatten each one completely against (or into) the wall surface. A good clinch sits flush with the wall or just barely proud of it — smooth, flat, and not sharp to the touch. A proud clinch that stands above the wall surface will rise further with time and eventually snag things or lacerate the opposite leg.
5
Run a Rasp Lightly Over the Clinch Line
A light pass of the rasp over the clinch line smooths any remaining sharp edges and embeds the clinches slightly into the wall surface. Do not rasp aggressively here — you are finishing, not removing material. The clinch line should now be a smooth, even row at consistent height across the wall.
6
Test the Shoe
Grip the shoe at the toe with both hands and try to wobble it. A correctly nailed and clinched shoe should have no movement at all. Any detectable wobble means a nail is not fully seated or a clinch is not holding. Identify the loose nail — it will often be visibly rising already — and drive and re-clinch it before finishing.

The Most Common DIY Mistakes

These are the errors that consistently separate DIY work that helps from DIY work that causes problems.

  • Pulling a shoe without cutting the clinches first. The single most damaging beginner error. Always cut every clinch before attempting shoe removal.
  • Removing too much sole. The sole is protective. An aggressive trim that thins the sole exposes the coffin bone to bruising on hard ground. Trim the wall, not the sole.
  • Uneven medial-lateral balance. Trimming more from one side than the other alters the horse's loading patterns. Check from behind frequently and stop if you're uncertain.
  • Using the wrong nail size. Too large and the nail won't drive cleanly; the head may not seat. Too small and it won't clinch adequately. Match to your farrier's specification.
  • Not supporting the foot properly. Trying to nail while the horse shifts weight onto the foot you're working on produces crooked nails and frustrated owners. A hoof stand or a helper makes a significant difference.
  • Driving nails straight in. Nails must angle slightly outward to exit the wall at the correct location. Straight nails go deep and either hit sensitive tissue or don't exit where needed.
  • Skipping the notch before clinching. Clinches without notches rise quickly and become sharp and dangerous.
  • Working on a horse that isn't cooperating. A horse that won't stand quietly for the farrier will not stand quietly for you. Do not struggle with a reluctant horse — you will make errors and risk injury. Call your farrier.
  • Continuing past uncertainty. If you're not sure what you're looking at, if something seems wrong, or if the horse is reacting — stop. Call your farrier. The value of DIY work is in routine situations where everything is straightforward. Stop the moment it's not.

Emergency Situations — What to Do Right Now

These are the scenarios where an owner needs to act before the farrier arrives.

Sprung or Loose Shoe

A shoe that is partially detached but still hanging on is more dangerous than a missing shoe — it can catch on things and tear a large section of hoof wall off. If a shoe is visibly loose, partially pulled, or sprung outward at a heel, pull it off completely following the steps above, apply a hoof boot, and call your farrier. Do not tape a loose shoe back on.

Risen Clinch

A clinch that has risen above the wall surface is sharp and can lacerate the opposite leg. If a clinch is visibly proud of the wall — you can feel it with your finger as a sharp projection — hold the clinch block against the nail head and knock the clinch back flat with the hammer. This buys time until the farrier can reset the shoe properly. Check all clinches at the same time — if one has risen, others may have as well.

Lost Shoe — Barefoot on One Foot

Apply a hoof boot immediately. Limit the horse to soft ground until the farrier arrives. If no boot is available and the horse must move on hard ground, keep movement minimal and on the softest available surface. Do not pack the hoof with anything unless directed by your farrier — well-intentioned packing with mud or manure can introduce bacteria to an already stressed hoof wall.

Nail Sticking Out

If a nail is protruding from the outside of the hoof wall — either driven incorrectly or partially worked loose — it must come out. Grip the nail head with pull-offs and pull it straight out along the angle it entered. Do not wiggle or lever it sideways — this enlarges the nail hole. After removal, the horse should be assessed for lameness before any further work. A nail that has been working loose for a while may have caused irritation; a nail driven incorrectly may have touched sensitive tissue.

Keep Your Farrier's Number Accessible

Not in a contacts list buried in your phone — physically on the barn wall or in a clearly labeled place accessible to anyone who helps at your barn. Farrier emergencies don't always happen when the primary owner is present. Anyone caring for your horse should know who to call and have the number they can reach immediately.
