Cutting & Cow Horse

Cutting and working cow horse demand a combination that pure reining does not — traction for explosive lateral movements alongside the ability to slide stops. The shoeing balance between grip and slide is more nuanced than any single-discipline sport.

← Back to Shoeing by Discipline

The Traction–Slide Balance

A cutting horse must stop, turn, and accelerate in fractions of a second to match a cow's movement. That requires enough hoof grip to push off laterally without slipping — but a horse that can't slide at all in a stop will jar joints and lose competitive marks. The shoeing solution is a moderate sliding plate on the hinds — narrower and shorter than a pure reining plate (typically ¾ to 1 inch wide vs. 1–1½ inch for reining) — combined with enough surface texture to allow some traction during the working phase.

  • Hind plate width: ¾ to 1 inch — narrower than reining, more grip built in
  • Front shoe: Fullered with toe or quarter clips — traction is needed for lateral work
  • Common addition: Toe clips on fronts are near-universal to prevent lateral shifting
  • Borium: Occasionally used on front heel calks for grip on hard or slick arena dirt

Working Cow Horse (Reined Cow Horse)

The NRCHA working cow horse sport adds a fence run and boxing phase to a reining pattern — meaning the horse must perform both disciplines in the same go. The shoeing must therefore accommodate both. Most NRCHA competitors shoe identically to cutting horses on the fronts and use reining-style sliding plates on the hinds. The compromise is that the sliding plate is shorter than a pure reining plate — providing slide capability without the extreme length that would be unsafe during the speed and direction changes of the cow work phase. The farrier's judgment in plate selection for working cow horse horses is particularly important because no single configuration is optimal for both phases — the horse and farrier find the best available compromise.

  • Key difference from reining: Horse must also handle fence work and cattle — requires more versatile shoeing
  • Plate approach: Shorter heel extensions than pure reining; sometimes a ¾-inch plate where reining would use 1¼ inch
  • Front priority: Maximum traction — the horse must push off hard laterally during cow work
