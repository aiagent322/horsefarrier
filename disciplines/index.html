Horse Shoeing
by Discipline

Every sport asks something different of the hoof — traction here, sliding there, breakover precision, durability over rough country. These guides break down how farriers shoe horses for the specific demands of each discipline.

Shoeing Is Not One-Size-Fits-All

A reining horse that slides forty feet, a jumper that must never slip on takeoff, an endurance horse covering a hundred miles of rock — each places completely different demands on the hoof and the shoe. These guides explain how farriers approach shoeing for each discipline: the priorities, the shoe types and modifications, the traction-versus-slide trade-offs, and what owners and riders should understand about their horse's feet for the sport they compete in.

Discipline Shoeing Guides — Nine Sports Covered in Depth
Reining
The most specialized hind shoeing in western performance. The sliding stop demands slick, wide sliding plates on the hinds that let the horse slide twenty to forty feet, while the fronts keep traction to steer the spin.
Western Performance
→
Cutting & Cow Horse
A different balance than pure reining — traction for explosive lateral stops and turns on the front end, combined with hinds that can still slide and give. Quickness and grip matter more than distance.
Western Performance
→
Barrel Racing & Rodeo
Traction, durability, and shoe security on unpredictable outdoor surfaces — packed arena dirt, grass, gravel. Rim shoes and traction devices keep the horse planted through tight, high-speed turns.
Speed & Rodeo
→
Dressage
Precision over drama. Hoof balance and breakover affect every movement a judge scores, so dressage farriery is about meticulous symmetry, correct medio-lateral balance, and clean breakover for expressive, even gaits.
English Sport
→
Show Jumping
Traction is the dominant concern — a slip on takeoff or landing is a safety crisis. Studs and traction shoeing are balanced against concussion management for a horse repeatedly absorbing the shock of landing.
English Sport
→
Eventing
The most demanding discipline for farriery — one horse must perform across dressage, cross-country, and show jumping, each with different footing and traction needs. Stud setups and durable, secure shoeing carry the day.
English Sport
→
Thoroughbred Racing
The most specialized farriery in the equine world. Race-plate farriers fit lightweight aluminum plates at speed and volume, optimizing every gram for horses running at the edge of physical limits.
Racing
→
Trail & Endurance
The widest range of terrain of any discipline — rocky mountain trail, deep mud, sand, pavement, water. Durability, protection, and grip over long miles drive choices like shoes with pads, or hoof boots.
Distance & Trail
→
Draft & Work Horses
Physically demanding, technically specialized, and underrepresented in mainstream farrier training. Massive horses, heavy shoes, and the realities of pulling and working surfaces define this corner of the craft.
