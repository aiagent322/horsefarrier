Top 10 Hoof Issues
for Horse Owners

The most common hoof problems you will face — with complete guides covering what each condition is, how your farrier treats it, when to call your vet, and what you can do at home every day.

Know What You're Dealing With

Most hoof problems are predictable, preventable, and treatable — if you catch them early and know which role belongs to whom. Each guide in this series covers the farrier's approach, the veterinarian's role, and the daily steps every horse owner can take.

Complete Guides — Issues 1 Through 10
1
Thrush
The most common hoof infection — bacterial invasion of the frog's sulci. Entirely preventable, easily treated when caught early, and potentially serious if ignored.
Mild to Serious Very Common
→
2
White Line Disease
Fungal and bacterial invasion of the white line zone — the junction between hoof wall and sole. Can silently undermine large sections of hoof wall before the horse shows any lameness.
Mild to Severe Common
→
3
Laminitis & Founder
Inflammation of the sensitive laminae — one of the most serious and painful hoof conditions a horse can develop. Requires immediate veterinary attention. Fast recognition changes everything.
Serious Common
→
4
Navicular Syndrome
Degeneration of the navicular bone and surrounding structures — a leading cause of forelimb lameness in performance horses. Manageable for years with the right farrier and veterinary team.
Serious Common
→
5
Cracked Hooves
From superficial grass cracks to deep quarter cracks that bleed and cause lameness. Six crack types explained with farrier repair techniques, infection management, and prevention.
Mild to Serious Common
→
6
Hoof Abscesses
Sudden severe lameness from a pocket of pressure-building infection inside the hoof. One of the most dramatic yet most treatable causes of acute foot pain — sound to three-legged lame overnight.
Moderate Common
→
7
Contracted Heels
Progressive narrowing of the heel region — a root cause of chronic thrush, abscesses, navicular syndrome, and poor performance. Fully reversible with patient farrier rehabilitation over 9–18 months.
Moderate Common
→
8
Seedy Toe
A hollow separation between the hoof wall and white line — often a consequence of laminitis or chronic wet conditions. How farriers identify and repair it.
ModerateComing Soon
9
Sheared Heels
Uneven heel bulb height caused by imbalance — one heel pushed higher than the other, creating asymmetric loading and long-term soundness problems.
ModerateComing Soon
10
Canker
A chronic hypertrophy of the horn-producing tissue — rarer than thrush but far more serious. Requires aggressive farrier and veterinary co-management.
